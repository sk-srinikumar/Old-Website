Background

I’m researching a cost-efficient (super cheap) multi-layered microfluidic chip production process. A paper from the University of Minho (Portugal) inspired me. They figured out the cheapest process in the world to produce single-layered chips. Based on their research, I’m figuring out an even cheaper process to produce multi-layered chips. Multi-layered chips have microscopic machinery in them while single-layered chips do not. Engineering and building microscopic machines is tricky.

My research is ongoing, but it is close to completion. Once completed, I will write a paper and share it on this site. But for now, enjoy all the pictures I took while conducting this research project!

This is a multi-layered microfluidic chip modeled by Stanford University. We buy these chips from a third-party manufacturer. Not only are these chips expensive, many of them arrive with defects. Add that with the fact we can only use them about a dozen times each, and you got a huge money sink.

To save money, I proposed we manufacture our own chips. Usually you need an expensive facility with expensive machinery solely for chip production. But I figured out a feasible manufacturing process.

This is the CAD file of the Stanford chip. My colleagues use these chips to experiment with microbes. It works well, but it’s not designed for that.

I designed a new chip schema. This one is easier to use and lasts longer.

To create the chip, we make a mold. To make the mold, we ordered a high resolution ( 2400 DPI) printer. We’re thinking about ordering one with an even higher resolution.

AutoCAD sucks at handling transparent microscopic designs like this. But I figured out a way to barely make it work. The print job pictured here is not small enough, but it got close.

I printed the schema onto porous transparent paper. But only one side is porous. This was the wrong side!

And this was the right side!

That paper with the schema gets made into a stencil. The schema is the right size here. This stencil will transfer the schema onto a mold.

I use special glass slides as a base for the mold. Glass cutting is surprisingly easy and fun with the right tools!

I’m preparing the subjects of my experiment by labeling them.

This is an SU-8 25 epoxy produced by Microchem. IBM invented and used it to make computer microchips. This epoxy will form the microscopic details of the mold.

This what a drop of that epoxy looks like on a petri dish.

That drop is spun flat.

Here’s the drop on the machine that will spin it.

Here I am testing if the spin coater machine is properly balanced. Turns out it wasn’t. I later built a gyroscopic contraption on top of the machine to fix the balance. It worked beautifully!

I can't expose the epoxy to light because it will start cross-linking. Basically, it becomes a crystal like a diamond or salt if it bathes in light too long. So I have to work in the dark.

I spun the epoxy flat on glass. Then the epoxy on glass in soft-baked on a hot-plate. I found out ceramic hot-plates don’t distribute heat evenly from the center. So don’t make the mistake I’m making in this picture!

We soft-bake instead of hard-baking (baking in an oven) because we utilize airflow. Fast airflow on a surface removes air-pressure, which lets things lift off easier. That’s why airplanes fly! Here, we want the water from the epoxy to evaporate faster.

I'm exposing the epoxy to UV light. The stencil limits which parts of the epoxy get UV exposure. The UV crystallizes all the epoxy it touches.

This area is the fume hood. It has a glass door, a chimney and other safety precautions. I’m working with smelly and dangerous chemicals, so I have to do most of my work in this tiny spot.

The epoxy here cracked badly. How you handle the epoxy depends on the lab’s temperature, humidity and equipment. I found out we can’t rely on manufacturer instructions because our lab conditions are wonky.

Here’s a completed mold. But why doesn’t it look clean? What’s deforming it? Eventually I figured it out...

I just finished conducting an experiment to isolate the problem. The entire batch of molds came out super deformed. Turns out the UV exposure machine is too old to do its job right.

I'm bathing the mold in an organic solvent to remove the non-crystallized epoxy.

This oxygen plasma machine is used on the chip itself, not the mold. The electricity pulls the electrons of the chip to the surface. Since the electrons are on the surface, the chips bonds to things without using glue. This is how we combine multiple layers to make a multi-layered chip. Glass is put under this machine to enable bonding too.
