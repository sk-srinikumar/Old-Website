Background

A company named PainChek contracted me to design a logo for their company and product. Their product is an app (also called PainChek) that scans a patient’s face and accurately reports their pain levels. Patients aren’t good at describing their pain, so it’s better to scan facial cues to determine their pain levels yourself. PainChek aims this app at medical professionals and caretakers.

Design

The logo looks like the letter P, but that’s not intentional. I experimented with sketching a smartphone in front of a person’s face. Then to represent data, scanning, and the flow of digital information, I sectioned the phone into a flow of rectangles. I pushed up the abstraction until it became what we have now.

I categorized PainChek as a medical utility company. Therefore, the logo needed a grandfatherly presence while feeling slick and technological. It needed to feel like a modern evolution of the IBM logo. I think I achieved that.

I tried dressing the logo in colors that made sense. Blue felt authoritative, green feels helpful, and the gray felt utilitarian. I immediately decided on a mix of teal and bright green. It oozes possibility and experience.

A cool thing about this logo is that it can incorporate sub-branding for other products. PainChek was considering branching the product out at the time of designing this, so they were happy that this is possible.

The founder of PainChek used to work at Roche, the makers of AccuChek. If you’re a diabetic, you probably know of AccuChek. I thought it would be neat to incorporate that history into the logo typeface. AccuChek’s logo typeface has a hand-written vibe to it. So I used that idea to create a typeface that kinda looked like a digital marker wrote it.
