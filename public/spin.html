Background

A spin coater is a machine that spins thick fluids so fast that they flatten to a height of only dozens of microns high! I designed a spin coater for flattening SU-8 25 epoxy through numerous stages of spinning.

Decent spin coater machines can cost a few grand. Because of budget cuts stemming from the executive branch of the U.S. government, funds became tight. This allowed me to design and build a spin coater for less than 100 bucks. So I got to designing a spin coater that’s not only cheap but also built for my research purposes. The wood-shop professor on campus agreed to build me the chassis for free. The doctor I work under already had soldering equipment. It wasn’t difficult at all to keep the costs low.

Once I designed the machine, I ordered $50 worth of parts including a DC motor, a power ]supply, and an H-Bridge. I planned to get all the core parts hooked up and running. Then I could order a custom PCB based on my amateur circuitry job. Finally, I’d put together the electronics, chassis and finishing touches. Well... that ’t happen.

Right after the parts arrived, my supervisor ended up buying a expensive spin coater for my research. My enthusiasm for my research, the faculty’s increasing interest in my project and the value of time convinced my supervisor that it was worth the money to purchase a spin coater. Anyways, hit me up if you wanna buy some cheap parts.

Design

Spin coaters on the market all have two things in common: those awful buttons and displays. Who wants to be pressing gummy buttons all day? It may look clean but it limits functionality. Various processes end being programmed through a limited window of buttons. Adding to the headache are cheap displays that never show the whole picture.

I prefer separate dials for each adjustable setting. It’s a lot of dials but dials are dirt cheap. I would love to have a separate display for each setting too, but displays are not dirt cheap. Since each adjustable setting has such a huge range of options, I thought it would make more sense to not add number labels around the dials. They’re cramped, imprecise and difficult for me to add. Instead, one display at the top of the machine would show every change for every setting.

You may think that’s a bad idea. How would someone know what they set each dial to if there’s only one display? Isn’t that inconvenient? Not in this case. My research includes analyzing spin processes. Meaning I’ll have every single setting recorded and updated on paper in front of me at all times. If somebody moves a dial by accident and nobody was aware, it may end up being extra work for me.

I restricted all interactive items to the sloped area. Sloped areas are ideal because the angle lets you see all the labels.

In the sea of dials, there is a lone button on the bottom right. I thought it was pointless to give it a label. If a user is looking for the machine’s start button, they’ll know which one it is. Cause it’s the only button there.

I never figured out where to put the power button. I thought it was something I could figure out later. At first, I wanted it in the sloped area because it’s an interactive object. But the power button is also an indicator like the display.

The dials are chronologically ordered from left to right. Each column of dials falls under the proper label for specific phases. I won’t benefit from this ordering. But if someone were to continue my research or produce microfluidic chips using my process, the organizing of the dials would help the user understand the process much faster. Also, it just feels right.

Painting each column would emphasize the order. If I made the chassis of aluminum and glass, different intensities of frosting on glass instead of paint would mark the columns. Maybe it would be different intensities of metal brushing. If I had money, this machine would end up in a museum of Braun products.

I initially planned on painting the entire chassis, but I realized it would be better to leave most of the wood unpainted. In a lab with many chemicals, you need to know what everything is made of so accidents can be dealt with properly.

Some issues are unpreventable. Some epoxy splashes everywhere when you spin at 3000 RPM; there’s no avoiding it! The spinning area incorporates a CD cake box lid for covering. A CD cake box lid is secured into the 3 curved rectangular mechanisms in the spinning area. Once epoxy build-up cakes the lid, we can throw the lid away and replace it with another dirt cheap CD cake box lid. It’s much cheaper to buy a new lid instead of cleaning the epoxy off the old one.

The part of chassis containing the spinning area is circular. Why is it circular and not square like the other side? Because... this is the spinning area!

The circular part of the chassis does not slope while the area next to it does. This will lead weird hand formations when adjusting the dial at the top-right corner. It’s fine because that specific setting hooked up to the dial rarely needs any change.

I originally wanted to place the spinning area to the north of the display, but that led to an undesirable shape and weight distribution. This final long rectangular design is easier to store and use in the lab I work in.

There are 3 LED lights sunk into the sides of the spinning area. These will light up while spinning is in progress. 3000 RPM is really fast. It’s fast enough to shoot thin glass through your eyeball and into your brain. The epoxy is spun and flattened on a glass plate which is secured to the spinning metal disc only by double-sided scotch tape. The cakebox will usually stop any flying glass, but you should still be aware of the potential.

It’s important to keep the spinning stable to produce the desired product and to reduce accidents. The legs at the bottom of the machine aren’t just legs. They’re rubber stabilizers that handle vibrations.

Overall, I’m proud of this design. The height comes out to about 3 inches; the length comes out to about 15 inches and the width comes out to about 6 inches. The dials, button, display, etc. are all scaled after parts I planned on purchasing. Let me know if you use this design to build your spin coater!
